Common shoulder conditions treated.

Soft-tissue problems, arthritis, instability and trauma can overlap. The evaluation helps sort out what is actually driving pain and loss of function.

💪

Rotator Cuff Tears

Pain, weakness, difficulty lifting the arm and night pain. Tears may be traumatic or degenerative.

🔥

Impingement & Bursitis

Pain reaching overhead, reaching behind the back or sleeping on the shoulder.

🎣

Biceps Tendinitis & Rupture

Pain in the front of the shoulder, pain with lifting, or a visible “Popeye” deformity after rupture.

🦴

Shoulder Arthritis

Glenohumeral arthritis and AC joint arthritis can cause stiffness, pain and loss of function.

🔁

Labral Tears & Instability

Bankart tears, bony Bankart injuries, recurrent dislocations and feelings of slipping or apprehension.

🏈

AC Separations & AC Pain

AC joint arthritis, separations and pain at the top of the shoulder after injury or overuse.

🩻

Clavicle Fractures

Collarbone fractures from falls, sports injuries, cycling crashes and other trauma.

🦾

Proximal Humerus Fractures

Fractures near the ball of the shoulder, often after a fall or higher-energy injury.

🎯

Scapular Dyskinesia

Abnormal shoulder blade mechanics that can contribute to pain, weakness and performance problems.

Understanding the shoulder.

The shoulder is a highly mobile joint. Pain may come from the joint surface, tendons, labrum, biceps tendon, AC joint, bone or shoulder blade mechanics.

Rotator Cuff

The tendon group that helps lift, rotate and stabilize the arm.

Labrum

A cartilage rim that helps deepen the socket and can tear with instability.

Biceps Tendon

A common source of anterior shoulder pain and inflammation.

AC Joint

The small joint at the top of the shoulder that can develop arthritis or separation.

Glenohumeral Joint

The main ball-and-socket shoulder joint affected by arthritis and instability.

Clavicle & Humerus

Bone injuries can range from collarbone fractures to proximal humerus fractures.

Shoulder procedures.

These cards use patient-friendly illustrations to show the problem, the repair concept and the goal of surgery when surgery is the right choice.

Arthroscopic tendon repair

Rotator Cuff Repair

Rotator cuff repair is used for selected tears that cause pain, weakness, night pain and loss of overhead function. The goal is to reattach the torn tendon to bone so it can heal.

  • Often performed arthroscopically through small incisions
  • Protects the repair early, then gradually restores motion and strength
  • Recovery depends on tear size, tissue quality and healing biology
Minimally invasive shoulder surgery

Shoulder Arthroscopy

Shoulder arthroscopy uses a camera and small instruments to treat selected rotator cuff, labral, biceps, bursitis, impingement and instability problems.

  • Camera-guided surgery through small portals
  • Can allow targeted treatment with less soft-tissue disruption
  • The specific procedure depends on the actual pain generator
Instability repair

Labral Repair & Stabilization

Labral repair may be recommended for selected Bankart tears, bony Bankart injuries, recurrent dislocations or persistent shoulder instability.

  • Goal is to restore stability and reduce recurrent dislocation risk
  • Return to sport depends on healing, strength and sport demands
  • Some instability patterns require more than a soft-tissue repair
Anterior shoulder pain treatment

Biceps Tenodesis

Biceps tenodesis treats selected painful biceps tendon problems by moving the tendon attachment away from the inflamed or unstable portion of the shoulder.

  • May be considered for biceps tendinitis, tearing or instability
  • Can be combined with rotator cuff or labral procedures
  • Different from tenotomy, which releases without formal fixation
Arthritis reconstruction

Shoulder Replacement

Shoulder replacement may be recommended for advanced arthritis or complex shoulder disease when pain, stiffness and function do not improve with nonsurgical care.

  • Includes anatomic and reverse shoulder replacement options
  • Implant choice depends on arthritis pattern, cuff status and anatomy
  • Goal is pain relief, better sleep and functional restoration
Fracture alignment and healing

Clavicle Fracture Surgery

Some clavicle fractures heal well without surgery. Displaced, shortened or unstable fracture patterns may benefit from plate-and-screw fixation.

  • Goal is to restore alignment and promote reliable healing
  • Fixation can allow earlier shoulder motion in selected cases
  • Healing and return to activity depend on fracture pattern and biology

All surgery has risks, including pain, bleeding, infection, damage to surrounding tissues, stiffness, blood clots, anesthesia risk, need for additional surgery, implant or fixation failure, heart attack, stroke and death. These risks are low, but they are not zero.

Frequently asked questions.

These answers are educational and do not replace a personalized evaluation.

Do all rotator cuff tears require surgery?

No. Some tears can be treated with activity modification, therapy, medication or injections. Surgery becomes more reasonable when pain, weakness, tear pattern, patient goals and imaging support repair.

What causes shoulder pain at night?

Night pain can occur with rotator cuff disease, bursitis, arthritis, frozen shoulder, biceps tendon problems and other conditions. Persistent night pain deserves evaluation.

What is shoulder impingement?

Impingement describes painful pinching or irritation in the shoulder, often involving the rotator cuff and bursa. It is usually treated first with therapy, activity changes and sometimes injections.

What is a Bankart tear?

A Bankart tear is a labral injury commonly associated with shoulder dislocation. A bony Bankart includes a piece of bone from the socket.

What is the difference between a shoulder separation and dislocation?

A separation is usually an AC joint injury at the top of the shoulder. A dislocation means the ball of the shoulder joint came out of the socket.

When is shoulder replacement recommended?

Shoulder replacement is considered when arthritis or complex shoulder disease causes significant pain, stiffness and loss of function despite appropriate nonsurgical care.

How long is recovery after rotator cuff repair?

Recovery varies by tear size, repair quality and patient factors. It typically involves a protection phase, gradual motion, strengthening and a longer return-to-activity progression.

Can PRP help shoulder pain?

PRP may be discussed for selected tendon or soft-tissue problems, but it is not a guaranteed treatment and is often not covered by insurance. See the orthobiologics page for more detail.
