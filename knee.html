Knee symptoms that deserve a clear orthopedic diagnosis.

Patients do not always know whether they have arthritis, a meniscus tear, ligament injury, cartilage problem, bursitis, Baker's cyst or fracture. They usually know what knee pain is preventing them from doing.

🚶Knee pain with walkingArthritis, meniscus tears, cartilage problems or tendon pain may contribute.
🪜Pain on stairsCommon with arthritis, patellofemoral pain, kneecap tracking issues or weakness.
💧Swelling or knee effusionSwelling after activity or injury can help guide the diagnosis.
🔒Locking, catching or clickingMay involve a meniscus tear, loose body or cartilage flap.
↘️Giving way or instabilityCan be related to ACL injury, patellar instability, weakness or pain inhibition.
⬅️Pain behind the kneeBaker's cysts, swelling, arthritis or meniscus pathology may contribute.
🔁Kneecap slippingPatellar instability can cause apprehension, swelling and recurrent dislocation.
🩻Fall, twist or collisionFractures, tendon ruptures, ligament tears and meniscus injuries should be evaluated.

Common knee conditions treated.

Knee pain can come from bone, cartilage, meniscus, ligaments, tendons, bursa, swelling or referred sources. The evaluation helps identify the main driver of pain and function loss.

🦴

Knee Arthritis

Osteoarthritis, post-traumatic arthritis and inflammatory patterns can cause pain, stiffness, swelling and activity limitation.

⚙️

Meniscus Tears

Twisting injuries or degenerative tears may cause joint-line pain, swelling, catching, locking or pain with squatting.

🏃

ACL Tears

Pivoting injuries, sudden deceleration, falls or sports injuries may cause swelling, instability and difficulty returning to cutting activities.

🔁

Patellar Instability

Kneecap dislocations, subluxations, MPFL injuries and recurrent apprehension with sports or daily activity.

🔥

Bursitis

Prepatellar bursitis, pes anserine bursitis and irritation from kneeling, overuse or inflammation.

💧

Baker's Cysts

Posterior knee swelling often associated with arthritis, meniscus pathology or underlying joint effusion.

🔒

Loose Bodies

Cartilage or bone fragments can cause catching, locking, swelling or motion blocks.

🌊

Knee Effusions

Swelling may come from arthritis, meniscus tears, ligament injury, cartilage injury, fracture or inflammation.

🧩

Cartilage Injuries

Focal cartilage defects, chondral flaps, patellofemoral cartilage wear and osteochondral injuries.

🩻

Fractures Around the Knee

Patella fractures, tibial plateau fractures, distal femur fractures and proximal tibia/fibula injuries.

🧵

Tendon Ruptures

Quadriceps tendon and patellar tendon ruptures can make it difficult or impossible to actively straighten the knee.

🎽

Overuse & Sports Knee Pain

Patellar tendinitis, IT band pain, runner's knee, jumper's knee and training-related symptoms.

Understanding the knee.

The knee is a complex hinge joint built for strength, motion, shock absorption and stability. Pain may come from the joint surface, meniscus, ligaments, kneecap, tendons, bursa or bone.

Articular Cartilage

The smooth joint surface affected by arthritis and cartilage injuries.

Meniscus

A shock absorber that can tear with twisting, squatting, sports or degeneration.

ACL & PCL

Central stabilizing ligaments important for knee stability and pivoting.

MCL & LCL

Side stabilizing ligaments that can be injured with twisting or contact.

Patella & MPFL

Kneecap tracking and stability structures involved in dislocation patterns.

Quadriceps & Patellar Tendons

The extensor mechanism needed to straighten the knee and climb stairs.

Treatment options

Start with the right diagnosis, then build the plan.

Most knee problems deserve a thoughtful discussion of both nonsurgical and surgical options. The right plan depends on the diagnosis, severity, imaging findings, activity goals, age, health and response to prior care.

✓ Topical and oral anti-inflammatories when appropriate
✓ Bracing, physical therapy, strengthening and return-to-activity planning
✓ Cortisone, hyaluronic acid and orthobiologic injections in selected patients
✓ Arthroscopy, ligament reconstruction, tendon repair, fracture fixation or knee replacement when indicated

Knee procedures.

Procedure recommendations are individualized after exam, imaging review and a discussion of goals, nonsurgical alternatives and recovery expectations.

Knee Arthroscopy

Minimally invasive camera-guided knee surgery may be used for selected mechanical symptoms, loose bodies, cartilage flaps or meniscus problems.

  • Small incisions and camera-guided treatment
  • May include loose body removal or chondroplasty
  • Not every arthritis problem benefits from arthroscopy

Meniscus Repair or Partial Meniscectomy

Some tears can be repaired. Other non-repairable tears may require trimming when symptoms remain mechanical and limiting.

  • Repairability depends on tear pattern, location and tissue quality
  • Recovery differs between repair and trimming
  • Arthritis changes affect decision-making

ACL Reconstruction

For selected patients with instability, pivoting demands, athletic goals or associated injuries where reconstruction best supports function.

  • Graft choice and rehab plan are individualized
  • Return to sport requires strength, motion and confidence
  • Meniscus and cartilage injuries may alter recovery

MPFL Reconstruction

For recurrent patellar instability, kneecap dislocations or subluxation patterns after anatomy and risk factors are evaluated.

  • Goal is to improve kneecap stability
  • Some patients require additional alignment procedures
  • Return to sport depends on healing and strength

Tendon Repair or Reconstruction

Quadriceps tendon and patellar tendon ruptures may require repair or reconstruction, especially when active knee extension is disrupted.

  • Often requires bracing and protected motion
  • Chronic injuries may need reconstruction
  • Recovery requires staged strengthening

Fracture Fixation

Fractures around the knee may require plate and screw fixation, intramedullary nail fixation or other methods depending on fracture pattern.

  • Patella, tibial plateau, distal femur and proximal tibia injuries
  • Fixation depends on stability, displacement and soft tissues
  • Recovery often includes protected weight bearing

Surgical risks

All surgery has risk, including pain, bleeding, infection, damage to surrounding tissues, stiffness, need for additional procedures, blood clot, implant or fixation failure, tendon or ligament failure, heart attack, stroke and death. These risks are low, but they are not zero.

Knee FAQ.

Common questions from patients with knee pain, swelling, arthritis and sports injuries.

Do I need an MRI for knee pain?

Not always. X-rays, exam findings, swelling, injury history and mechanical symptoms help determine whether MRI is useful.

How do I know if my knee pain is arthritis or a meniscus tear?

They can overlap. Arthritis often causes stiffness, aching, swelling and activity-related pain. Meniscus tears may cause joint-line pain, catching, locking or pain with twisting.

When is knee swelling concerning?

Rapid swelling after injury, inability to bear weight, fever, redness, severe pain, deformity or loss of motion should be evaluated.

Can cortisone help knee arthritis?

Cortisone may reduce inflammation and pain for selected patients, but it is not a cure and should be used thoughtfully.

What is hyaluronic acid?

Hyaluronic acid is a gel-like injection option used for selected arthritis patients depending on symptoms, severity and coverage.

Can PRP help knee pain?

PRP may be considered for selected tendon, ligament, cartilage or arthritis-related conditions, but it is not appropriate for every patient. Learn more on the Orthobiologics page.

When is knee replacement reasonable?

Usually when arthritis is advanced, symptoms meaningfully limit life, imaging supports the diagnosis, and nonoperative care no longer provides acceptable relief.

Does the robot do the knee replacement?

No. Robotic assistance is surgeon-controlled. It helps with planning and precision, but the surgeon performs the operation.

Can meniscus tears heal without surgery?

Some can improve with nonoperative care. Repairability depends on tear pattern, location, tissue quality, age, activity goals and associated injuries.

When is ACL reconstruction needed?

It is considered for patients with functional instability, pivoting demands, athletic goals or associated injuries where reconstruction best supports function.

What are red flags after a knee injury?

Inability to bear weight, deformity, rapid swelling, inability to straighten the knee, major instability, fever, redness, calf swelling or severe worsening pain should be evaluated urgently.

How long is recovery?

Recovery depends on diagnosis and treatment. Injection, therapy, arthroscopy, ACL reconstruction, tendon repair, fracture fixation and knee replacement all have different timelines.
