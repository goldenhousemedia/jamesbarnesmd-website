Orthobiologics patient education

PRP and Bone Marrow Concentrate

Orthobiologics use carefully prepared biologic substances from your own body — most commonly platelet-rich plasma (PRP) or bone marrow aspirate concentrate (BMAC) — to support the body’s healing response in selected orthopedic conditions.

Schedule AppointmentRead FAQs

Is orthobiologic treatment right for you?

The answer depends on your diagnosis, imaging, activity goals, prior treatments, and whether your tissue or joint still has the capacity to respond.

PRP and BMAC are not magic cures, and they are not a substitute for every surgery. They are tools that may help selected patients with tendon problems, mild to moderate arthritis, and certain soft tissue conditions when used as part of a comprehensive orthopedic plan.

The most important question is not simply, “Can I get PRP?” It is: “What is my actual diagnosis, and which treatment has the best chance of helping me reach my goal?”

Understanding the PRP process.

PRP begins with a blood draw and ends with a targeted injection designed to support your body’s healing biology.

What is PRP?

PRP stands for platelet-rich plasma. A sample of your blood is processed to concentrate platelets and growth factors. The resulting PRP may then be injected into a targeted joint, tendon, or soft tissue area when appropriate.

  • Usually performed as an outpatient procedure.
  • Commonly considered for selected tendon problems and mild to moderate arthritis.
  • Different PRP preparations can behave differently, which is why technique and indication matter.
  • Anti-inflammatory medication planning is important around treatment.

Candidate selection matters.

PRP tends to make the most sense when there is a treatable biologic problem and realistic expectations.

Better candidates

PRP may be considered for mild to moderate arthritis, chronic tendon pain, tennis or golfer’s elbow, rotator cuff tendinopathy, patellar or Achilles tendinopathy, plantar fasciitis, and selected ligament or soft tissue problems.

Less ideal candidates

PRP is less predictable for advanced bone-on-bone arthritis, severe deformity, complete tendon ruptures, active infection, certain blood disorders, or conditions that clearly require surgery.

PRP is different from cortisone.

Cortisone and PRP are both injections, but they are used for different reasons and with different expectations.

Cortisone can reduce inflammation quickly and is often covered by insurance. PRP is generally aimed at supporting a healing response and may be considered when the goal is longer-term tissue or symptom improvement rather than rapid short-term relief.

Most insurance plans do not routinely cover PRP. Patients should expect PRP may be self-pay unless their plan specifically states otherwise.

PRP vs. BMAC.

Both come from your own body, but they are not the same treatment.

Bone Marrow Aspirate Concentrate

BMAC is made by aspirating bone marrow, usually from the pelvis, and concentrating it. It contains platelets, signaling proteins, and marrow-derived cells that may influence inflammation and tissue healing.

  • More involved than PRP because bone marrow must be harvested.
  • May be considered in selected arthritis, cartilage, tendon, or biologic augmentation situations.
  • Should not be marketed as a guaranteed “stem cell cure.”
  • Usually self-pay for many orthopedic uses.

Coverage, cost, and medication planning

Most insurance plans do not routinely cover PRP or BMAC for many orthopedic conditions. Coverage rules vary by plan and can change, but patients should expect that orthobiologic treatments may be self-pay.

Medication planning matters. Anti-inflammatory medications can interfere with the desired biologic response, and blood thinners may affect bleeding risk around blood draw or bone marrow harvest. Medication changes must be individualized and coordinated safely.

Orthobiologics FAQ

Common questions patients ask about PRP, BMAC, expectations, medication holds, and safety.

What is PRP?

PRP stands for platelet-rich plasma. A small sample of blood is drawn and processed to concentrate platelets and growth factors. The PRP is then injected into a targeted joint, tendon, or soft tissue structure when appropriate.

What is BMAC?

BMAC stands for bone marrow aspirate concentrate. Bone marrow is usually taken from the pelvis and processed to concentrate marrow-derived cells, platelets, and signaling proteins. It is more involved than PRP and should be used selectively.

Is this the same as stem cell therapy?

Not exactly. BMAC contains a small number of marrow-derived cells along with many other biologic factors. It should not be described as a guaranteed stem cell cure or as a treatment that regrows a normal joint.

Does insurance cover PRP or BMAC?

Often no. Many insurance plans consider PRP and BMAC investigational or non-covered for orthopedic uses. Patients should expect these treatments may be self-pay unless their plan states otherwise.

Do I need to stop NSAIDs before or after PRP or BMAC?

Yes, in most cases Dr. Barnes recommends an NSAID holiday for approximately two weeks before and two weeks after orthobiologic treatment. This includes common anti-inflammatory medications such as ibuprofen, naproxen, meloxicam, diclofenac, and similar medications unless you are specifically instructed otherwise. If you require NSAIDs for another medical reason, discuss this before scheduling treatment.

Do I need to stop blood thinners or anticoagulants?

Some patients may need a temporary anticoagulant or blood-thinner plan around the time of the blood draw or bone marrow harvest, but this must be individualized. Do not stop aspirin, Plavix, warfarin, Eliquis, Xarelto, Pradaxa, or any other blood thinner unless Dr. Barnes and the prescribing clinician agree it is safe. For some patients, stopping anticoagulation is more dangerous than proceeding with treatment, so coordination is essential.

How long does it take to work?

Improvement, when it occurs, is usually gradual. Some patients notice changes over weeks, while others may take several months. Some patients do not improve enough to consider the treatment successful.

Will PRP or BMAC help me avoid surgery?

Sometimes these treatments may delay or reduce the need for surgery, but not always. If there is advanced arthritis, severe deformity, instability, or a major tendon tear, surgery may still be the most predictable option.

Are PRP and BMAC safe?

Because PRP and BMAC are made from your own blood or marrow, allergic reaction risk is generally low. However, any injection or aspiration can cause soreness, bleeding, infection, nerve irritation, flare reaction, or failure to improve symptoms.

Can I get cortisone instead?

Sometimes. Steroid injections can reduce inflammation quickly, but they are not the same as PRP or BMAC. The best choice depends on the condition, timing, tissue quality, arthritis severity, and your goals.

Do I need imaging guidance?

For many injections, ultrasound or fluoroscopic guidance can improve accuracy. Dr. Barnes will discuss whether image guidance is appropriate for your specific condition.

What should I bring to the appointment?

Bring X-rays, MRI reports, prior injection records, therapy notes, surgery records, and a list of medications and supplements. This helps determine whether PRP, BMAC, standard injection therapy, therapy, or surgery is most appropriate.
