          Treatment pathway.

          Most athletes deserve a plan that starts with the correct diagnosis and escalates treatment only when appropriate.

          1

          Evaluation

          History, physical exam, imaging review and sport-specific goals.

          2

          Nonoperative Care

          Activity modification, bracing, topical or oral NSAIDs, physical therapy and injections when appropriate.

          3

          Rehabilitation

          Strength, mobility, balance, progressive loading and safe return-to-sport milestones.

          4

          Surgery When Needed

          Arthroscopic or open procedures for selected patients when symptoms, goals and injury pattern support surgery.

          Common Sports Medicine procedures.

          Surgery is considered for selected patients when nonoperative options are not enough or when the injury pattern is unlikely to recover adequately without repair.

          Knee Arthroscopy

          • Meniscus repair or partial meniscectomy
          • Loose body removal
          • Cartilage evaluation and treatment

          ACL Reconstruction

          • Instability after pivoting injury
          • Return-to-sport planning
          • Structured rehabilitation progression

          Shoulder Arthroscopy

          • Rotator cuff repair or debridement
          • Labral repair for instability
          • Biceps tenotomy or tenodesis
          • Subacromial decompression/acromioplasty

          Elbow & Tendon Surgery

          • Tennis elbow surgery for selected patients
          • Biceps tendon rupture evaluation
          • Tendon repair or reconstruction when appropriate
          About surgical risk: surgery risks include pain, bleeding, infection, damage to surrounding tissues, need for additional procedures, bone failure, implant failure, soft tissue failure, blood clot, heart attack, stroke and death. These risks are low, but they are not zero.

          Watersports Medicine.

          Lake Havasu is a major watersports destination. Falls at speed, repetitive shoulder loading, rope traction and high-energy impacts can create shoulder, knee, elbow, hand and fracture injuries.

          • Wakeboarding, waterskiing, wakesurfing and jet skiing
          • Shoulder instability, labral tears and rotator cuff injuries
          • Knee ligament injuries and meniscus tears
          • Hand injuries, lacerations and fractures

          Desert Sports Medicine.

          The desert around Lake Havasu supports UTV riding, dirt bikes, mountain biking, trail running and hiking. These activities can generate both overuse problems and higher-energy injuries.

          • Clavicle and distal radius fractures
          • Shoulder separations and dislocations
          • ACL tears, meniscus tears and ankle fractures
          • Achilles ruptures and tendon overuse injuries

          Sports Medicine FAQ.

          Common questions from athletes, parents and active adults.

          Can I play through my injury?

          Sometimes, but not always. Pain that changes mechanics, causes instability, swelling, weakness, numbness, loss of motion or worsening performance should be evaluated before continuing.

          Do I need an MRI?

          Not every injury needs an MRI. X-rays, exam findings and symptom pattern often guide the next step. MRI is considered when the diagnosis is unclear or when results would change treatment decisions.

          What is the difference between a strain and a tear?

          A strain is an injury to muscle or tendon. A tear can be partial or complete. Severity depends on location, tissue involved, strength loss, function and imaging findings.

          Can a meniscus tear heal without surgery?

          Some meniscus symptoms improve without surgery, especially degenerative tears. Some traumatic tears or mechanical symptoms may need arthroscopy. Treatment depends on tear pattern, age, activity goals and exam findings.

          Can PRP help my sports injury?

          PRP may be considered for selected tendon, ligament, cartilage or joint conditions. Insurance coverage is variable and often limited. Learn more on the Orthobiologics page.

          What causes tennis elbow?

          Tennis elbow is usually an overuse tendinopathy of the common extensor tendon. It can occur from tennis, pickleball, weightlifting, repetitive gripping, tools and daily activities.

          What injuries are common in pickleball?

          Common pickleball injuries include tennis elbow, rotator cuff pain, Achilles tendinitis, calf strains, ankle sprains, meniscus flares and knee arthritis symptoms.

          What injuries are common in wakeboarding and watersports?

          Falls and rope traction can cause shoulder instability, labral tears, rotator cuff injuries, knee ligament injuries, hand injuries, fractures and lacerations.

          What injuries are common in UTV and desert sports?

          Desert sport injuries may include clavicle fractures, wrist fractures, shoulder separations, ankle fractures, ACL tears, meniscus tears and more complex trauma.

          When should I see a Sports Medicine Orthopedic Surgeon?

          Schedule an evaluation if pain limits activity, symptoms persist, you cannot return to sport, there is swelling or instability, or you are concerned about a tendon, ligament or cartilage injury.
