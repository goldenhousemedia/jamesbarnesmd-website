          Youth athlete care

          School sports injuries, return-to-play decisions, and sideline-informed care.

          As one of the team physicians for Lake Havasu High School, Dr. Barnes understands the pressure athletes, parents, coaches, and trainers feel after an injury. The goal is to identify the problem, protect the athlete, and build a safe path back to sport.

          • ACL and meniscus injuries
          • Shoulder instability and labral injuries
          • Ankle sprains and stress fractures
          • Patellar instability and tendonitis
          • Throwing and overuse injuries
          Active adult Sports Medicine

          Care for pickleball players, golfers, lifters, hikers, cyclists, swimmers, and active retirees.

          Staying active is not optional for many Lake Havasu patients. Treatment is built around function: less pain, better motion, better strength, and realistic decisions about therapy, injections, orthobiologics, or surgery.

          • Tennis elbow and golfer's elbow
          • Rotator cuff pain and bursitis
          • Degenerative meniscus tears and knee arthritis
          • Achilles, patellar, quadriceps and biceps tendonitis
          • Hip bursitis, gluteal tendon pain, and walking/hiking pain
          Desert sports injuries

          Orthopedic care for Havasu's trails, desert terrain, and off-road lifestyle.

          Desert sports bring a mix of overuse injuries and trauma. Care may involve X-rays, MRI, bracing, therapy, injections, fracture care, or surgery depending on the injury pattern and the patient's goals.

          • Clavicle and distal radius fractures
          • Shoulder separations and instability
          • ACL tears, meniscus tears and tibial plateau fractures
          • Ankle fractures and Achilles ruptures
          • Trail running and hiking overuse injuries
          Water Sports Medicine

          Care for the lake injuries that happen when recreation gets intense.

          Lake Havasu is one of the premier watersports destinations in the Southwest. Falls, rotational injuries, dislocations, and high-speed crashes can involve the shoulder, knee, hand, wrist, ankle, or spine.

          • Shoulder dislocations and labral tears
          • Rotator cuff injuries and AC separations
          • Knee ligament injuries and meniscus tears
          • Finger, hand and wrist injuries
          • Fractures, lacerations and boating-related trauma

          How treatment is usually approached.

          Sports Medicine treatment is not one-size-fits-all. The right plan depends on the diagnosis, imaging, activity goals, timing, age, health status, and whether the injury is likely to heal without surgery.

          1

          Evaluation

          History, examination, imaging review and diagnosis.

          2

          Nonoperative care

          Activity modification, therapy, bracing, topical/oral NSAIDs and injections when appropriate.

          3

          Recovery plan

          Rehabilitation, strength restoration, sport-specific progression and monitoring.

          4

          Surgery when needed

          Arthroscopy or repair may be appropriate for selected injuries and goals.

          Surgical risks

          All surgery carries risk, including pain, bleeding, infection, damage to surrounding tissues, need for additional procedures, failure of bone, implant, or soft tissue healing, blood clot, heart attack, stroke and death. These risks are low, but they are not zero.

          Sports Medicine FAQ.

          Common questions from athletes, active adults and parents.

          Do I need surgery?

          Not necessarily. Many sports injuries improve with therapy, activity modification, bracing, medications, injections, or time. Surgery is considered when the injury pattern, symptoms, goals and failure of conservative care make it reasonable.

          When should I be seen after a sports injury?

          Seek evaluation promptly for inability to bear weight, deformity, major swelling, obvious instability, suspected fracture, numbness, weakness, or a joint that dislocated. Persistent pain or loss of function that is not improving also deserves evaluation.

          Can I play through pain?

          Sometimes mild soreness can be monitored, but sharp pain, instability, swelling, weakness, or worsening symptoms should not be ignored. Playing through the wrong injury can make recovery longer or cause additional damage.

          Do I need an MRI?

          Not always. X-rays and examination often guide the first step. MRI may be helpful when there is concern for meniscus tears, ACL injuries, rotator cuff tears, labral injuries, stress fractures, or tendon rupture.

          Can PRP help my sports injury?

          PRP may be considered for selected tendon, ligament, cartilage, or joint conditions. It is not appropriate for every injury, and most insurance plans do not cover it. Learn more on the Orthobiologics page.

          What is the difference between a sprain, strain and tear?

          A sprain usually involves a ligament. A strain usually involves muscle or tendon. A tear means fibers are disrupted and may be partial or complete.

          What injuries are common in pickleball?

          Tennis elbow, rotator cuff pain, Achilles tendonitis, calf strains, meniscus injuries, knee arthritis flares, ankle sprains and falls are common patterns.

          What injuries are common in watersports?

          Shoulder dislocations, labral tears, AC separations, rotator cuff injuries, knee ligament injuries, hand injuries, lacerations and fractures can occur with falls, ropes, boards, boats and personal watercraft.

          What injuries are common with UTV and desert sports?

          Fractures, shoulder separations, clavicle fractures, wrist fractures, ankle injuries, knee ligament injuries and soft tissue trauma are common concerns after crashes or falls.

          How do return-to-play decisions work?

          Return-to-play depends on healing, pain, strength, range of motion, balance, sport-specific function, risk of re-injury and whether the athlete can safely perform the demands of the sport.
