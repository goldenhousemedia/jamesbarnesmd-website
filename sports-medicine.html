Treatment Philosophy

1. Diagnose the real problem.

A good plan starts with understanding the injury pattern, exam, imaging, sport, timeline, and goals.

2. Use nonoperative treatment when appropriate.

Many sports injuries improve with time, therapy, bracing, training changes, NSAIDs when appropriate, injections, or orthobiologics.

3. Use surgery selectively.

Surgery may be the best option for selected patients with repairable tears, instability, mechanical symptoms, displaced fractures, or failed nonoperative care.

Surgery Risks

All surgery has risk. These risks are low, but they are not zero.

  • Pain
  • Bleeding
  • Infection
  • Damage to surrounding tissues
  • Need for additional procedures
  • Bone, implant, or soft tissue failure
  • Blood clot
  • Heart attack, stroke, or death

Sports Medicine FAQ

Common questions from athletes, parents, coaches, and active adults.

Do I have to be a competitive athlete to see a Sports Medicine Orthopedic Surgeon?

No. Sports Medicine also includes active adults, weekend warriors, workers, recreational athletes, and retirees who want to stay active.

Will I need surgery?

Not necessarily. Many injuries improve with activity modification, bracing, physical therapy, topical or oral NSAIDs when appropriate, injections, and time. Surgery is considered when the injury pattern and patient goals make repair or reconstruction reasonable.

When should an athlete be evaluated?

Evaluation is recommended for severe pain, deformity, inability to bear weight, recurrent instability, locking or catching, weakness, loss of motion, symptoms lasting more than expected, or pain that limits sleep, work, school, or sport.

Can Dr. Barnes help with return-to-play decisions?

Yes. Return-to-play decisions consider healing, pain, motion, strength, functional testing, sport-specific demands, season timing, and risk of reinjury.

What are common Lake Havasu sports injuries?

Common patterns include pickleball elbow and shoulder injuries, golf overuse injuries, watersport shoulder injuries, UTV/off-road fractures, high school football knee and shoulder injuries, baseball/softball throwing injuries, and fitness-related tendon injuries.

What is the role of PRP or BMAC?

PRP and BMAC may be considered for selected tendon, ligament, cartilage, and joint conditions. These are not a cure-all and are not appropriate for every injury. Learn more on the Orthobiologics page.

Is cortisone bad for athletes?

Cortisone can be helpful for selected inflammatory conditions, but timing, diagnosis, dose, and sport demands matter. Some tendon problems may not be ideal for repeated steroid exposure. The decision should be individualized.

How long does recovery take after knee arthroscopy?

Recovery varies by procedure. A simple partial meniscectomy often recovers faster than a meniscus repair or ligament reconstruction. Sport-specific recovery may take weeks to months depending on the procedure and goals.

How long does recovery take after rotator cuff repair?

Rotator cuff repair recovery often takes several months. Tendon healing is slow, and return to heavy lifting, sports, or overhead activity depends on tear size, repair quality, rehab progress, and strength recovery.

How long does recovery take after ACL reconstruction?

Many athletes require 9–12 months before unrestricted cutting, pivoting, or contact sports. Timelines vary based on graft choice, associated injuries, strength, confidence, and functional testing.

Do you provide sideline coverage?

Yes. Dr. Barnes provides sideline coverage for local sports and serves as a team physician for Lake Havasu High School.

What should I bring to my appointment?

Bring imaging discs or links, MRI reports, X-ray reports, prior operative notes, injection records, therapy notes, bracing information, and a list of current medications.
