Why Do Joints Pop and Crack?

Most painless popping is common and not dangerous. The important question is whether the sound comes with pain, swelling, locking, instability, or a recent injury.

Bone BlogGeneral OrthopedicsPatient Education

Joints pop and crack for several reasons, and most of the time, painless popping is not dangerous. If a joint makes noise but does not hurt, swell, lock, or feel unstable, it is usually not something to panic about.

The story changes when the popping is painful, happens after an injury, or comes with swelling, weakness, catching, locking, or loss of motion. In those situations, it may be worth having the joint evaluated.

Why Do Joints Make Noise?

Joints are not silent machines. They are living structures made of cartilage, ligaments, tendons, joint fluid, bone, and surrounding soft tissues. As those structures move, they can sometimes create sounds.

The most common joint sounds are described as popping, cracking, clicking, snapping, grinding, or creaking. Those words may sound concerning, but the sound itself is not always the problem. What matters more is whether the sound is associated with pain or dysfunction.

Gas Bubbles in Joint Fluid

One common reason joints pop is a process called cavitation. Many joints contain synovial fluid, which helps lubricate the joint. As the joint moves or stretches, pressure inside the joint can change. That pressure change can allow tiny gas bubbles to form or collapse, creating a popping or cracking sound.

This is one reason people can crack their knuckles. The sound can be loud, but in many cases it does not mean the joint is being damaged. The old belief that cracking your knuckles automatically causes arthritis is not well supported. That said, if cracking a joint causes pain, swelling, or loss of motion, that is different from painless habitual cracking.

Tendons and Ligaments Moving Over Bone

Another common cause of popping is soft tissue movement. Tendons and ligaments can move over small bony prominences as a joint bends and straightens. When the tendon shifts position, it may create a snapping or clicking sensation.

This can happen around the hip, knee, ankle, shoulder, wrist, or fingers. Sometimes this is harmless. But if the snapping becomes painful, irritated, or limits activity, then it may represent tendon inflammation, instability, or a mechanical issue that deserves attention.

Rough Joint Surfaces and Arthritis

Some joint noises come from rougher joint surfaces. Healthy cartilage is smooth and allows the joint to glide. With arthritis, cartilage can thin or become irregular. That can create grinding, crunching, or creaking sensations. This type of sound is sometimes called crepitus.

Crepitus does not automatically mean someone needs surgery. Many people have noisy arthritic joints and manage well with activity modification, strengthening, anti-inflammatory strategies, injections, or other non-surgical treatments.

When Is Joint Popping Usually Not a Big Deal?

Joint popping is usually less concerning when it is painless, has been present for a long time, is not associated with swelling, does not cause locking or instability, and does not limit daily activities. In those cases, the sound may simply be part of how that joint moves.

When Should You Be More Concerned?

You should consider getting evaluated if joint popping or cracking is associated with:

  • pain
  • swelling
  • catching or locking
  • giving way or instability
  • decreased range of motion
  • weakness
  • bruising after an injury
  • a new pop after a fall, twist, or sports injury
  • worsening symptoms over time

For example, a painless pop in the knee while stretching is usually a different situation than a loud pop during a twisting injury followed by swelling and difficulty walking.

What Can You Try First?

If the popping is painless and the joint works normally, you may not need to do anything. If there is mild discomfort, reasonable first steps may include avoiding movements that repeatedly irritate the joint, gentle stretching, gradual strengthening, improving mobility around the joint, using ice after activity if there is soreness, and modifying exercise volume or technique.

Over-the-counter anti-inflammatory medication may help some people, but it is not appropriate for everyone. Patients with kidney disease, stomach ulcers, blood thinner use, heart disease, or medication interactions should be especially careful and ask their clinician first.

How I Generally Evaluate This in Clinic

When a patient comes in for joint popping or cracking, I usually start with the story. I want to know which joint is involved, how long it has been happening, whether it is painful, whether it started after an injury, whether there is swelling, and whether the joint catches, locks, or gives way.

Then I examine the joint. I check motion, strength, tenderness, stability, swelling, and specific patterns that may point toward a tendon, ligament, cartilage, arthritis, or nerve-related issue.

X-rays can be helpful when arthritis, alignment, fracture, or bone-related problems are suspected. An MRI is not always needed for joint noise alone, but it may be considered if there are signs of a soft tissue injury, persistent mechanical symptoms, or symptoms that do not improve with appropriate treatment.

Treatment Options

Treatment depends on the cause. For painless popping, reassurance may be all that is needed. For irritated tendons or overuse problems, treatment may include activity modification, physical therapy, stretching, strengthening, bracing, or anti-inflammatory strategies.

For arthritis-related grinding or crepitus, treatment may include exercise, weight management when relevant, anti-inflammatory medication, injections, bracing, and activity changes. Surgery is usually considered only when symptoms are persistent, functionally limiting, and not responding to reasonable non-surgical treatment.

Bottom Line

Joints pop and crack for many reasons, and painless popping is usually not dangerous. The sound itself is less important than the symptoms that come with it.

If your joint pops but feels strong, moves well, and does not hurt, it may simply be normal joint mechanics. But if popping comes with pain, swelling, locking, instability, or starts after an injury, it is worth getting checked.

A noisy joint does not always mean something is wrong, but a noisy and painful joint deserves a closer look.
