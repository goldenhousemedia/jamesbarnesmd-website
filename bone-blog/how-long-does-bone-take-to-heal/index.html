How Long Does It Take for a Bone to Heal?

A simple orthopedic explanation of fracture healing timelines, what affects recovery, and when a broken bone should be checked.

Bone BlogFracture CarePatient Education

Quick answer

Most broken bones take about 6 to 8 weeks to heal enough for basic daily activity, but that is only a general rule. Some small fractures may heal faster, while larger or more complicated fractures can take several months or longer.

Bone healing is not just a clock. It depends on the bone involved, the fracture pattern, alignment, stability, blood supply, the patient’s health, and the treatment plan.

What does “healed” actually mean?

When patients ask how long a bone takes to heal, the answer depends on what we mean by “healed.” A bone may be stable enough on x-ray before the patient feels completely normal. Returning to walking, work, lifting, or sports can each have a different timeline.

Stiffness, soreness, swelling, weakness, and loss of endurance can last longer than the early bone-healing phase itself. That does not automatically mean something is wrong, but it does mean recovery is more than just waiting for the bone to bridge on x-ray.

How bone healing happens

Bone is living tissue. When it breaks, the body starts a repair process. First, bleeding and inflammation occur around the fracture. Then the body forms a soft healing tissue called callus. Over time, that callus hardens into new bone. Finally, the bone remodels and strengthens over months.

That remodeling phase is why some fractures can technically “heal” in weeks but continue to improve for months.

General bone healing timelines

Every fracture is different, but common general timelines include:

Small bones, such as fingers or toes, may heal in about 3 to 6 weeks.

Many wrist, ankle, collarbone, and arm fractures often take about 6 to 10 weeks.

Larger bones, such as the tibia, femur, pelvis, or more complex fractures, may take 3 months or longer.

Stress fractures are also variable. Some mild bone stress injuries improve in several weeks, while more severe or higher-risk stress fractures can take longer.

Why some bones heal slower than others

Several things can affect bone healing. The first is the location of the fracture. Some bones have better blood supply than others. Good blood flow helps healing. Poor blood flow can slow healing.

The second is the fracture pattern. A simple, nondisplaced fracture usually heals more predictably than a fracture that is displaced, unstable, crushed into multiple pieces, or open through the skin.

The third is stability. Bones heal best when the broken pieces are lined up well and protected enough to allow new bone to form. That may be done with a splint, cast, boot, brace, or surgery depending on the injury.

Patient factors matter too. Smoking or nicotine use, diabetes, poor nutrition, certain medications, osteoporosis, infection, and high-energy injuries can all affect healing.

What are signs a bone may be healing?

In general, patients often notice pain gradually improving. Swelling may decrease. The injured area may feel more stable. They may need less pain medication. They may slowly tolerate more motion or weight-bearing if their doctor allows it.

Symptoms alone do not prove that a fracture has healed. X-rays are often used to check alignment and healing progress.

What patients can usually do to help healing

The biggest thing is to follow the weight-bearing and activity instructions given by the treating clinician. Doing too much too soon can delay healing or cause the fracture to shift.

Basic steps include protecting the injury as instructed, keeping follow-up appointments, avoiding nicotine, eating enough protein and calories, making sure vitamin D and calcium intake are adequate when bone health is a concern, and doing motion exercises only when cleared to do so.

For many fractures, physical therapy may be helpful after the initial healing period to restore motion, strength, balance, and confidence.

When should you see an orthopedic specialist?

You should be evaluated if you have significant pain after an injury, difficulty bearing weight, visible deformity, worsening swelling, numbness, tingling, an open wound near the injury, or pain that is not improving as expected.

It is also worth seeing an orthopedic specialist if you were told you have a fracture and want to understand whether it is stable, whether it needs surgery, when you can return to activity, and what the follow-up plan should be.

How I would generally evaluate this in clinic

In clinic, I would start with the story: how the injury happened, where it hurts, whether the patient can bear weight, and whether there are any numbness or circulation concerns.

Then I would examine the injured area, including swelling, tenderness, motion, stability, skin condition, and nerve and blood flow status.

X-rays are usually the first imaging test. Sometimes repeat x-rays are needed because some fractures become easier to see with time. In certain cases, CT or MRI may be needed, especially for complex fractures, joint fractures, stress fractures, or injuries that do not show clearly on x-ray.

Treatment options

Many fractures can be treated without surgery using a splint, cast, walking boot, brace, sling, or activity modification. The goal is to protect the bone while it heals in an acceptable position.

If the fracture is unstable, badly displaced, involves a joint, fails to heal, or is likely to heal in a poor position, surgery may be recommended. Surgery may involve screws, plates, rods, pins, or other fixation to hold the bone in better alignment while healing occurs.

Surgery does not make healing instant. It usually improves alignment and stability, but the bone still has to biologically heal.

Bottom line

For many patients, a broken bone takes about 6 to 8 weeks to heal enough for basic function, but the full recovery may take longer. The safest answer depends on the specific bone, the fracture pattern, the x-rays, and the patient.

If you have a fracture or pain that is not improving, getting it evaluated does not mean you automatically need surgery. It means you get a clear diagnosis, a timeline, and a plan to heal as safely as possible.

Need a fracture checked?

If an injury is limiting your activity or your recovery is not progressing as expected, an orthopedic evaluation can help clarify the diagnosis and next steps.
